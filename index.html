Still a Man’s World

A closer look at twenty years of the Pictures of the Year competition reveals a gender crisis.

by Deb Pang Davis

Background

What is the Pictures of the Year competition?

Pictures of the Year, started in spring of 1944 as the First Annual Fifty-Print Exhibition by Cliff Edom at the University of Missouri. At the time, its purpose was “to pay tribute to those press photographers and newspapers which, despite tremendous war-time difficulties, are doing a splendid job to provide an opportunity for photographers of the nation to meet in open competition; and to compile and preserve… a collection of the best in current, home-front press pictures”.

The next few years welcomed the beginning of a series of changes in name and scope that also mark the genesis of the competition’s evolution as society and the industry transformed. In 1948 the Fifty-Print Exhibition became the News Picture of the Year Contest when magazine photographers were invited to participate. When the University of Missouri and the National Press Photographers’ Association (NPPA) merged contests in 1957, the competition became Pictures of the Year (POY). Known today as POYi (Pictures of the Year International), it is one of the oldest photojournalism contests. Thousands of images are entered by photographers from around the world. (Pictures of the Year | 77th Annual Competition | History)

Reframing Photographs investigates the Pictures of the Year competition archives — photos and information about winners — between 1984 and 2004. Phase one digs into the data about the POY winners to understand the representation of women and men within the archives.

Why 1984 to 2004? This timeframe was a period of immense change both for the competition, the publishing industry and systems surrounding photojournalism. It was, according to Bill Garrett, former Editor of National Geographic, “the Oscars of photojournalism” (source).

The Jury

Well-respected scholars, photographers, editors, and designers have typically invited to be on the jury. As the competition continues to evolve to reflect the industry, the jury evolves as well. Recruitment and selection of jury members is a subjective process and an art.

Bill Kuykendall:

Every judge must be able to speak authoritatively and convincingly using specifics and not just feelings. My NPPA liaisons and I put a lot of effort into identifying prospective judges who had the right combination of processional experience as [photographers] and editors and the ability to articulate the virtues and shortcomings of entries. Temperament is another quality. The [panel] requires individuals who 'play well' with others.

Historically, the POY jury has been all male.

Yet, during Bill Kuykendall’s tenure, female representation started to become a higher priority. According to Bill, “every effort was made to have at least one jury member from outside the United States” and the voices of female judges were highly valued. Women “had a better sense of proportion and understood how to compromise while remaining steadfast champions of [exceptional] work”.

The Photographers (Award Winners)

For many years, photo competitions offered the rare outlet for photographers to have their work rewarded and recognized with the hope their work is seen by people who hire and/or publish photography. Winning an award in POY tended to be a valuable stepping-stone for photographers’ careers. Newspaper and magazine photographers also saw the Pictures of the Year competition as a way to earn respect from the ‘word people’ who ran their papers. As photography gained more respect, winning awards gave photographers more bragging rights, giving many photographers a type of fame and prestige within and out of the photojournalism community. Most were men, giving them more and highly coveted assignments and greater earning power (source?).

The Awards

Winning an award from the Pictures of the Year competition offered fuel for marketing and brand value for organizations. According to Bill Kuykendall, “publishers saw contests as a way to burnish their publications’ reputation. Picture editors saw them as a way to develop and motivate their staff”. Howard Chapnick of Black Star and Bob Gilka of National Geographic recognized the value of entering POY and other photo competitions, encouraging their photographers to enter and providing the time and resources to do so. Winning gave organizations promotional capital: Their staffs were among the best visual storytellers in the world.

According to David Rees, the scale and scope of the Pictures of the Year changed dramatically during Bill Kuykendall’s tenure. The number of entries increased to tens of thousands, [increase in sponsorships? exhibitions?]

A total of 3,803 awards were given to photographers between 1984 and 2004.

Freelance Photographers

Naturally, as the more traditional model and markets for photojournalism showed signs of collapse, the traditional outlets for publishing photojournalism also diminished. Full-time newspaper jobs have declined dramatically since the 1990s (need to add annotation). Not surprisingly, there has been a steady increase of freelance photographers over time. Given these conditions, it would be natural to assume freelancers would be winning a greater percentage of awards each year.

Percent of Award Winners Who are Freelance Photographers

Between 1984 and 2004, a total of 477 awards were given to freelance photojournalists with a significant percentage peak in the mid-to-late 90s.

These percentages were derived from award winners designated as freelance or independent. This may or may not include freelancers who were contracted by wire services or photo agencies. Awards associated with an agency, wire service, newspaper or magazine, were counted with the publishing outlets.

Yet, beginning in 1999, the number of awards given to freelancers shows a decline. Why? A few possibilities:

In 2001, after a split with the National Press Photographers Association (NPPA) POY started to charge entry fees for the first time in its history.

2000 to 2004 brought major world events including the dot com bubble, Enron, 9/11 terrorist attacks, the War on Terror, the War in Afghanistan, and several coups, conflicts and civil wars around the world. Events considered “news” for U.S. publishing outlets meant sending photographers overseas to cover them which meant resources and safety concerns. Most freelance photographers did not have such resources unless contracted by major publishing outlets in the U.S. or Europe. It could be during this time, publishing titles were devoting greater amounts of resources to major news events.

Even by the end of 2004, photographers were still employed by many newspapers. Fewer than years before but many could still count on the benefits of full-time employment while also having the resources and time to publish meaningful work.

So, even though 2004 also marked the beginning of a sizeable decline in local newspapers and the appearance of news deserts, the most catastrophic shedding of staff photographer jobs began with the Great Recession.

Gender Representation of Award Winning Freelancers

Of the 477 number of awards awarded to freelancers, only 17% were won by women photographers between 1984 and 2004.

While the number of awards won by freelance photographers seem to have decreased between 2000 and 2004, by all other accounts, the number of freelance photojournalists were on the rise. Research shows that greater numbers of freelance photojournalists are women. They also tend to be more educated, use a diverse set of technology but suffer financially from erratic income and assignments. (Source: Hadland & Barnett).

If more women are freelance photographers, what are the possible reasons for the lower numbers and what is the impact for women in photojournalism? Do fewer women photographers enter contests? Why? Are fewer women photographers sent on assignment? How does the definition of “news” impact who gets assigned and ultimately who wins awards?

Why Are Women Still A Minority?

This glimpse into the Pictures of the Year competition reinforces what many of us already know: photojournalism is a male-dominated profession. It’s also safe to say women have had to confront circumstances, rise up to meet challenges and address societal expectations unique to their sex. Still, they have overcome those challenges, circumstances and expectations as well as those inherent to photojournalism regardless of gender, to produce some of the most compelling photographs of our time. Jesse Tarbox, Diane Arbus, Mary Ellen Mark, Susan Meiselas, Carol Guzy, Maggie Steber …

Women have endured. Women have and continue to have a voice. So, why are women still a minority when it comes to who gets assignments, who gets published, and who is represented in competitions? One question addressed during analysis: What type of relationship exists between women jurors and women winners? Between the years 1984 and 2004, there were none.

Given the noticeably dominant male perspective of the world through pictures and across centuries, it is natural to wonder how this nearly singular perspective impacts our perceptions of the world. In fact, researchers and practitioners alike have grappled with this question for decades in art, film, and more recently photojournalism.
