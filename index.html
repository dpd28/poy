Reframing Photographs

What does a closer look at twenty years of the Pictures of the Year competition reveal about gender?

by Deb Pang Davis

Background

What is the Pictures of the Year competition?

Pictures of the Year, started in spring of 1944 as the First Annual Fifty-Print Exhibition by Cliff Edom at the University of Missouri. At the time, its purpose was “to pay tribute to those press photographers and newspapers which, despite tremendous war-time difficulties, are doing a splendid job to provide an opportunity for photographers of the nation to meet in open competition; and to compile and preserve… a collection of the best in current, home-front press pictures”.

The next few years welcomed the beginning of a series of changes in name and scope that also mark the genesis of the competition’s evolution as society and the industry transformed. In 1948 the Fifty-Print Exhibition became the News Picture of the Year Contest when magazine photographers were invited to participate. When the University of Missouri and the National Press Photographers’ Association (NPPA) merged contests in 1957, the competition became Pictures of the Year (POY). Known today as POYi (Pictures of the Year International), it is one of the oldest photojournalism contests. Thousands of images are entered by photographers from around the world. (Pictures of the Year | 77th Annual Competition | History)

Reframing Photographs investigates the Pictures of the Year competition archives — photos and information about winners — between 1984 and 2004. Phase one digs into the data about the POY winners to understand the representation of women and men within the archives.

Why 1984 to 2004? This timeframe was a period of immense change both for the competition, the publishing industry and systems surrounding photojournalism. It was, according to Bill Garrett, former Editor of National Geographic, “the Oscars of photojournalism” (source).

Award winning Organizations

A total of 3,810 awards were given between 1984 and 2004. National Geographic, The Washington Post, and The Associated Press were the top three award-winning organizations.

Freelance Photographers

Between 1984 and 2004, a total of 478 freelance photographers won the most awards. Not suprisingly, this number has increased since the beginning of the competition as full-time jobs for photographers have decreased dramatically over the years.
